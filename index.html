Monty Hall Probability

The Monty Hall problem is a famous paradoxical problem in conditional probability.

In the problem, you are on a game show, being asked to choose between three doors. Behind each door, there is either a car or a goat. You choose a door. The host, Monty Hall, picks one of the other doors, which he knows has a goat behind it and opens it, showing you the goat. (You know, by the rules of the game, that Monty will always reveal a goat.) Monty then asks whether you would like to switch your choice of door to the other remaining door. You prefer having a car more than having a goat. Do you choose to stick or switch?

The program calculates the probability depending on the open-door event for the winning strategy between stick and switch. The minimum number of doors is 3. The difference between the total number of doors and the number of doors the host can open should be at least 2, to calculate probability between 2 doors. The program assumes that 1 of the doors is the user's choice.
This table is an example of possible input values for the total number of doors and min/max values of the doors the host can open.
